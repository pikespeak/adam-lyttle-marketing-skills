# Idea Validation Reference

Source: `sources/App Idea & Validation (Part 1 of 3).srt`

This reference distills Adam Lyttle's process for finding, testing, and refining app ideas before building.

## Operating Principle

Good app ideas come from living in the real world, listening to people, watching trends, and noticing opportunities. The first idea does not need to be grand. Start small, build something, and let the process reshape how you think.

The goal is not merely to find an interesting idea; it is to find an idea with a target market, proof that people search for it, a plausible distribution angle, and a simple enough scope to execute.

## Five-Step Idea Process

1. Find inspiration and set goals.
2. Form an early concept.
3. Validate technical feasibility with a proof of concept.
4. Validate market demand.
5. Refine or pivot the idea.

## Inspiration And Goals

Start with a concrete challenge rather than a vague revenue dream. Revenue can be a goal, but Adam emphasizes process goals that guide decisions:

- Release within two weeks.
- Reach a specific download milestone.
- Try to go viral on TikTok or X.
- Learn a skill.
- Improve at a specific part of the app cycle.
- Build something simple, fun, and mass-market.

Clear goals make it easier to judge ideas. If the goal is "simple, fun, potentially viral, not months of work," ideas that fail those constraints can be rejected quickly.

## Early Concept

Keep the first concept rough and limited to one or two core features. Avoid competitor research too early because it can flatten the concept into a copy of existing products.

Ask:

- What is the core interaction?
- Why would it be fun or useful?
- What would someone do in the first few seconds?
- Can this be explained simply?
- Does it connect to an existing trend, behavior, or demand?

Example from Swipe the Cat:

- Inspiration: viral thumb-swipe TikTok trend.
- Initial concept: make the trend interactive.
- Goal: simple, fun, mass-appeal, potentially viral.

## Technical Proof Of Concept

Before over-investing, build a quick prototype to see whether the mechanic works outside your head.

The proof of concept should answer:

- Can the core interaction be built?
- Does it feel fun or useful when touched?
- Does the concept become clunky in practice?
- Does it resemble an existing product too closely?
- Is there enough novelty to continue?

Adam's prototype used swipe-driven video scrubbing. It worked technically, but the first fruit theme felt boring and too close to Fruit Ninja. That finding was useful because it exposed the difference between technical feasibility and product appeal.

## Market Validation

Use keyword and trend data to refine direction.

Recommended research pattern:

- Use App Store keyword tools such as Astro to check popularity and difficulty.
- Filter by category, geography, and popularity.
- Look for themes people already search for.
- Check exact-match competition in top results.
- Use Google Trends to test whether demand exists outside the App Store.

Swipe the Cat pivot:

- Fruit slicing worked technically but lacked originality.
- Keyword research surfaced "cat game" and "cat games" as promising.
- Google Trends confirmed search interest outside the App Store.
- The theme changed from fruit slicing to cats because it created more playful interactions and stronger market alignment.

## Pivot Criteria

Pivot or abandon early when:

- The prototype works technically but is boring.
- The mechanic feels clunky.
- The theme is too close to a dominant competitor.
- Search demand is weak.
- Competition is too strong for the launch strategy.
- The idea no longer matches the original goal.

Stay flexible on the surface expression of the idea while protecting the original goal.

## Common Mistakes

- Looking only where other builders look.
- Starting with a huge idea instead of a small challenge.
- Letting competitor apps dictate the concept too early.
- Treating a working prototype as proof of market demand.
- Ignoring boredom or clunkiness in the proof of concept.
- Choosing a theme without checking search demand.
- Refusing to pivot after market data improves the idea.

## Planning Checklist

1. State the challenge goal.
2. Name the trend, behavior, or pain that inspired the app.
3. Define one or two core features only.
4. Build or request a proof of concept for the core mechanic.
5. Record what felt fun, clunky, boring, or derivative.
6. Research App Store keyword popularity and difficulty.
7. Check exact-match competition.
8. Check Google Trends or external demand.
9. Decide: continue, pivot theme, change mechanic, or abandon.
