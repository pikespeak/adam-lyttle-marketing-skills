# Launch And Marketing Reference

Source: `sources/Launch & Marketing (Part 3 of 3).srt`

This reference distills Adam Lyttle's launch and marketing process for solo app builders. Use it as tactical guidance, not as a universal guarantee.

## Operating Principle

The hard part is no longer only building the app; it is getting people to see and use it. Marketing starts before launch and before implementation because distribution depends on demand, keywords, positioning, and the target market.

Do not treat ASO as a post-build copy exercise. A weak pattern is: build an app, publish it, then try to reverse-engineer keywords afterward. The stronger pattern is: validate search demand and competition first, then build and position the product around that opportunity.

## ASO Model

Ranking depends on three ingredients:

- Keyword optimization.
- Reviews.
- Download velocity.

Keyword process:

1. Choose one primary keyword for most of the ASO effort. Adam describes focusing about 90% of ASO on one keyword at launch.
2. Pick the keyword that best balances relevance, conversion intent, and meaningful search popularity.
3. Put the exact primary keyword phrase in the app title with words adjacent and in order. Avoid separators, hyphens, or reordered words.
4. Use the subtitle for secondary terms, but write for humans. The wording should be descriptive and emotive, not keyword-stuffed.
5. Avoid repeating the same keyword word across title and subtitle when possible. Repetition may reduce effectiveness.

Example pattern:

- Primary keyword: `Cat game`.
- Title pattern: `Swipe the Cat game`.
- Secondary targets: `Cute game`, `Offline game`.
- Subtitle pattern: `Cute, Offline, Fun`.
- Reason: `game` is already in the title, while `Cute` and `Offline` add secondary combinations without repeating `game`.

## Recommended Search Effect

Exact-match title keywords can help the app appear in App Store recommended search suggestions shortly after launch. This can expose the app to people typing the target query before organic rankings fully settle.

Important timing:

- Recommended search listings can update within hours after the app goes live.
- Organic search rankings can lag by days, weeks, or longer depending on category and niche.
- The launch window is therefore a temporary visibility opportunity, not a durable ranking guarantee.

## Launch Visibility Campaign

Adam's narrow paid tactic is an Apple Search Ads visibility campaign, not a broad paid acquisition program.

Use when:

- The app has just been approved or gone live.
- The goal is to appear for the exact app title or brand/title keyword while App Store indexing catches up.
- The team can actively monitor spend.

Setup:

- Campaign name: `Visibility`.
- Target exact-match keywords for the app title and brand/title phrase.
- Turn Search Match off.
- Avoid broad match.
- Use a high enough CPT bid and daily cap to trigger impressions quickly, then monitor closely.
- Shut the campaign off once the app is indexed and ranking for the intended terms.

Guardrails:

- If spend moves too fast, check whether exact match was missed, broad match was used, or Search Match was left on.
- Do not let the platform find a generic audience for this campaign. The point is exact visibility for the app's own title/keyword.
- Make the budget advice contextual. Adam used aggressive caps and bids to force impressions, but the principle is speed plus monitoring, not reckless spend.

## Download Velocity

The first few days and weeks matter because early downloads appear to influence how the App Store understands the app's target market, popularity, and ranking trajectory.

Launch planning should answer:

- Where will the first downloads come from?
- What happens immediately when the app is approved?
- Which channels can send external traffic in launch week?
- How will downloads, rank, reviews, and ad spend be monitored?
- What is the fallback if one channel fails?

Every external download counts in launch week. Views and likes are secondary unless they produce installs.

## X And Community Launch

Social distribution is a long-term asset for solo developers. It works best when the builder has already invested in the community and built trust before launch.

Launch post structure:

- Builder/community hook: announce the app and set a public goal.
- User hook: explain the concrete product idea in a way normal users understand.

Example pattern:

- "New app is live."
- "I made [recognizable trend/problem] into a real [product category]."
- "Goal: reach [specific measurable target] by [date]."

Why it works:

- Other builders want to follow and support a public goal.
- The product hook gives non-builders a reason to care if the post breaks outside the builder bubble.

Launch-day behavior:

- Treat X like Product Hunt for a day.
- Aim for sustained timeline presence.
- Do not spam the same app link repeatedly.
- Post distinct angles: launch announcement, demo, personal context, competition, build tutorial, progress update, and behind-the-scenes artifact.
- Repost or quote the launch announcement when adding context so users can understand and download the app.

Risk:

- Launches are unpredictable. A platform outage, algorithm miss, or timing issue can break momentum.
- Do not rely on one post, one tweet, one reel, or one viral event.

## Word Of Mouth

Long-term low-cost growth comes from turning existing users into distribution.

Design shareable moments:

- Identify what screen, result, stat, creation, or achievement users naturally want to share.
- Make that moment screenshot-worthy.
- Add app branding, app name, and logo to the shared visual.
- Create an emotional pause before users continue.
- Add a share button when appropriate.

Swipe the Cat example:

- The game-over/high-score screen is the natural sharing point.
- It pauses the game loop and asks for user attention.
- It includes an award graphic, top score, branding, and logo.
- The goal is to make users feel attached to the score and want to share it.

Effect model:

- If 1 in 5 users share with one friend, that can create roughly 20% more downloads from a simple product moment.

## Common Mistakes

- Building first and only later looking for search demand.
- Targeting too many keywords at launch instead of concentrating on one primary keyword.
- Stuffing keywords into title/subtitle at the cost of human readability.
- Repeating important title words in the subtitle unnecessarily.
- Assuming recommended search equals organic ranking.
- Running launch ads with Search Match on or broad match enabled.
- Treating paid ads as the whole marketing strategy.
- Measuring social launch success only by views or likes rather than installs.
- Spamming the same launch message instead of creating distinct post angles.
- Depending on one viral post or one launch channel.
- Forgetting to build shareable product moments.

## Planning Checklist

Use this checklist when preparing a launch recommendation:

1. Validate demand: target market, search popularity, competition.
2. Choose the primary keyword and explain why it should convert.
3. Draft title with exact primary keyword phrase.
4. Draft subtitle with secondary keyword words and user-readable value.
5. Define review and rating collection path.
6. Define launch-week download sources.
7. Specify Apple Search Ads visibility setup and shutoff rules if relevant.
8. Write a goal-based launch post with builder and user hooks.
9. Create supporting post angles for the launch day.
10. Design at least one branded shareable moment inside the app.
11. Define monitoring: downloads, rank, spend, reviews, social traffic, conversion.
